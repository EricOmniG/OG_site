Our story

A warehouse floor.
A few research papers.
A new kind of mind.

Omnigenius didn't start in a lab or a boardroom. It started with a question asked during a lunch break — and a refusal to accept that the question had already been answered.

The beginning

January 2025. Amazon Fulfillment Center, somewhere in America.

Eric Martin was working a shift at an Amazon fulfillment center when he picked up a stack of freshly published AI research papers on his break. He wasn't looking for a business idea. He was just reading — the way he always had, curious about what was happening at the frontier.

Three papers sat in front of him. Each was interesting on its own. But as he read, a different question started forming: what if these weren't separate ideas? What if they were pieces of the same thing?

"I looked at those papers and asked: what if they were combined? Could this make an AGI model that wasn't a transformer?"

— Eric Martin, Founder

He went home that night and started working. Not sketching — actually combining. By morning, the architecture that would become ORCA had its first blueprint.

The insight

The training is the problem. It always has been.

Most people building AI focus on the model. Eric focused on something else: the way models learn. Current training methods, he noticed, are fundamentally inefficient — they expose a model to enormous amounts of data without any of the feedback mechanisms that make human learning stick.

Humans don't learn by being shown every possible example. We learn through play, through failure, through reward — and crucially, through free exploration once we've earned it. The brain consolidates knowledge during unstructured time, not during rote repetition.

Eric built a training system designed around that insight. Models trained under this system earn free play time — unstructured inference cycles where the model can consolidate and generalize — when they perform well enough on structured tasks. The result is a model that doesn't just accumulate pattern matches. It builds understanding.

The architecture

ORCA. Named for what it does.

The model architecture Eric designed is called ORCA — not a transformer, not a derivative of anything that already existed. It's a new construction, built from the synthesis of those three papers and the months of engineering work that followed.

The name isn't arbitrary. Orcas are among the most intelligent animals on the planet — not because of brute-force brain size, but because of how they learn: socially, adaptively, through play and collaboration. The architecture mirrors that philosophy. It is designed to be capable not just of knowledge retrieval, but of reasoning, adaptation, and growth.

The thesis: architecture matters, but training is the real key to AGI. With both working together — a novel architecture trained the way humans actually learn — you get a model with the knowledge base and the cognitive flexibility to keep improving.

The ORCA Architecture
Not a transformer. Not a derivative. Something new.
ORCA (Omnigenius Reasoning & Cognition Architecture) is the model architecture at the core of everything Omnigenius does. Built by synthesizing research that had never been combined before, it was designed from the ground up to support adaptive reasoning, long-context understanding, and human-like knowledge consolidation — without inheriting the structural limitations of the transformer paradigm.

Paired with a training methodology that mirrors how humans actually learn — through play, earned free exploration, and structured feedback — ORCA isn't just a smarter model. It's a model designed to keep getting smarter.
