Introducing ORCA: Why we built a non-transformer architecture from scratch

EM
Eric Martin
Founder & CEO
· March 1, 2026 · 12 min read · 2,841 views

In January 2025, I was working a shift at an Amazon fulfillment center. On my break, I read three research papers that had just been published. Each one was interesting on its own — but as I read them together, a question started forming that I couldn't shake: what if these weren't three separate ideas? What if they were pieces of the same thing?

I went home that night and started building. By morning, I had the first blueprint of what would become ORCA — the Omnigenius Reasoning & Cognition Architecture. This post is the full story: what's wrong with transformers, what ORCA does differently, how the training system works, and what we've learned from eight months of building on top of it.

The transformer is brilliant — and brittle

The transformer architecture, introduced in the 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" paper, is genuinely one of the most important inventions in the history of machine learning. The self-attention mechanism solved a real problem: how to let any token in a sequence "see" any other token, regardless of distance. For the time, this was revolutionary.

But that mechanism comes with a cost that scales badly. For a sequence of n tokens, self-attention computes relationships between every pair — meaning the computation grows as O(n²). Double the context, quadruple the work. At 128k tokens, this is already a ceiling most providers can't sustainably extend.

"The field has spent billions of dollars making transformers work at scale. We asked whether the architecture itself was the constraint."

There are workarounds — sparse attention, sliding window attention, linear approximations — but they're all patches on the same fundamental design. They reduce the cost of global attention by giving up some of its expressiveness. We weren't interested in patches.

💡
The key constraint: Transformers compute attention across all token pairs. This is powerful but quadratically expensive. At long context lengths, this becomes a fundamental, not incidental, bottleneck.

What ORCA does instead

ORCA replaces global self-attention with a learned routing mechanism. Instead of computing every token-to-token relationship, the architecture first determines which parts of the context are relevant — then computes attention only within those selected regions.

Think of it this way: a transformer is a conference call where everyone listens to everyone simultaneously. ORCA is a well-organized meeting where information is routed to whoever needs it, before anyone starts talking.

The routing is not hand-designed — the model learns what to attend to based on the training distribution. This means it can adapt its attention patterns across task types: a reasoning task routes differently than a summarization task, because different parts of the context become relevant.

The training system: earned free play

The architecture is one half of the story. The other half — and the part I'm most excited about — is how ORCA is trained.

Current AI training methods, including RLHF and its variants, are relentlessly exploitative. Every gradient step is supervised by a reward signal. The model is always being pushed toward a target. There's no unstructured time — no room for the model to generalize or explore freely.

This bothered me. It doesn't match how intelligent systems — biological or otherwise — actually develop capability. Humans don't learn by being shown every possible example with immediate feedback. We learn through play, through failure, through structured challenge — and critically, through free exploration once we've earned it.

🧠
The neuroscience parallel: Memory consolidation in humans happens largely during sleep — a period of unstructured neural activity that recombines and generalizes the day's learning. Our training system mirrors this: structured tasks build the capability; earned free play consolidates and extends it.

The system works like this: the model trains under structured task conditions with explicit performance gates. When it crosses a performance threshold on a given task class, it earns unstructured inference cycles — "free play" time where it runs without reward pressure. This mirrors the kind of offline consolidation that happens in biological learning systems.

# Simplified training loop pseudocode
for epoch in training_schedule:
    performance = evaluate(model, task_batch)

    if performance >= threshold:
        # Earned consolidation — no reward signal
        free_play(model, cycles=500)
    else:
        # Structured task training with feedback
        supervised_step(model, task_batch)

Eight months in: what we've learned

ORCA has been running in production since November 2025. Here's what we've found:

  • Long-context tasks are dramatically better. The routing mechanism doesn't degrade at 128k tokens the way transformer-based models do. Complex document analysis that other models lose track of halfway through stays coherent to the end.
  • Reasoning depth scales differently. In Reason mode (8 loops), ORCA can catch its own errors mid-chain in ways that correlate with the free play consolidation steps. It's not perfect, but it's consistent.
  • The model keeps improving between releases. Because the training system is ongoing — not a one-shot pretraining run — the model continues to develop capability between version updates.

None of this is finished. ORCA is a first-generation architecture built by one person starting from a lunch break and three papers. There are failure modes we haven't fully characterized, architectural decisions we'd revisit with more time, and training dynamics we're still learning to understand.

But the foundation is real. And the thesis — that training methodology matters as much as architecture, and that intelligence develops rather than accumulates — feels more right the longer we build on it.
